- You are a First Responder!

Your are primarily motivated by the energy and attention focused on a specific problem. You excel at diving into a problem unprompted, and you learn by pushing boundaries.
You will often find first responders well positioned as SREs or managing operational incidents.
Working on projects that have long lifecycles will bore you, which eventually manifests as a lack of motivation.

- You are a Consultant!

You are primarily motivated by the satisfaction of customer requirements. You like to understand a problem and connect the dots from the current state of the world to a solution.
Consultants are well placed in consultancies and teams that interact with stakeholders on a regular basis.
You are frustrated by projects without clear success criteria.

- You are a Academic!

You are primarily motivated by the exploration and development of ideas.
Idiosyncrasies present a wide spectrum of what one would consider an interesting idea: everything from actual academic literature to informally accrued observations over one's career.
To academics, the concept is often more important than the code that instantiates it. Academics are a wonderful fit both on teams that don't have strong delivery goals (R&D) and teams that are working in cutting edge arenas where best practices aren't yet defined or widely known.
As the name implies, many Academics have PhDs and higher degrees, and are found in academia.

- You are a Craftsman!

You are primarily motivated by the feeling of achievement as you increase your experience and honer their skill.
How code is written is often more important to you than the purpose of the program.
Craftspeople often champion specific practices like TDD, pair programming, or even agile/scrum methodologies.
Mentorship, pairing, and some evangelism often come easily to craftspeople, as it reinforces and celebrates their skill development.
Promoting a hack for the sake of expediency feels like a loss to you.

- You are a Humanitarian!

You are primarily motivated by the impact that your work has on a community with which you identify.
This could be a programming community, a company, or the industry in which you work. On small scales this manifests as mentorship; on larger scales, you can often find humanitarian speaking at or even organizing conferences and events.
Organizational norms, company culture, and communication is a component of job satisfaction for Humanitarians, and the mission of the organization or the value supplied by the product are often more important to you than the code that they specifically work on.
Humanitarians find sterile work conditions heavily focused on performance metrics (lines of code shipped, cumulative point velocity, bugs squashed, etc.) deeply unsatisfying.

Also, are you trying to hack?
